ReliefSignal is a local-first disaster relief coordination workspace built for the first 72 hours of response.
In this walkthrough, I will show how raw field updates become a live priority queue, a response brief, a checklist, and an outreach plan.

Most community responders already have the information they need.
What they do not have is a clean way to organize it fast enough.
Updates arrive from messaging groups, volunteers, clinics, shelters, and local leaders in one messy stream.
That makes it easy for urgent needs to get buried.

ReliefSignal starts with that raw intake.
It turns field notes and needs assessments into structured response signals.
Each signal is scored for urgency, impact, and effort so the queue surfaces what matters first.

From there, the workspace gives coordinators a clear response board.
Signals move from incoming to verified, mobilizing, and resolved.
That keeps the team focused on what is still unconfirmed, what is already in motion, and what has been closed.

ReliefSignal also maps the next 72 hours.
That timeline matters because responders need more than a list.
They need a believable operating rhythm they can explain to volunteers, shelter leads, clinics, and local officials.

Another important layer is the export.
The app produces a response brief, a coordination checklist, and a partner outreach plan from the same local state.
That makes the product useful beyond the screen itself.

Under the hood, the build stays intentionally practical.
ReliefSignal runs fully in the browser, stores the workspace locally, and supports offline usage with a service worker.
There is no backend dependency, no secrets, and no fragile demo setup.

This project fits the Airia AI Agents Hackathon because it shows a clear multi-agent coordination story in a high-pressure workflow.
The result is not just a concept.
It is a polished, inspectable product for real operational pressure.
